== The Core Idea

=== Problem Statement

Today's eyewear market creates several problems for consumers:

1. **High replacement costs**: When frames break, consumers must either:
   * Pay for a complete new pair (frames + new lenses)
   * Keep broken frames to preserve expensive prescription lenses
   * Wait for lens transfer, which is not possible when the frame model goes out of circulation

2. **Lens-frame incompatibility**: Each frame requires custom-cut lenses, making it impossible to:
   * Own multiple frame styles for the same prescription
   * Quickly replace broken frames
   * Share lenses between family members with similar prescriptions and sizes

3. **Waste and inefficiency**: Functional lenses are discarded when frames break, and functional frames cannot be used when prescriptions change

4. **Limited frame selection**: Consumers often choose frames based on availability at their optometrist rather than unlimited choice

5. **Complex logistics**: Eyeglass must align costumer's wishes, frame brands and lens manufacturers:

=== Proposed Solution

Create an **industry-wide standard** for interchangeable prescription lenses interfaces that:

* Use standardized lens sizes (UES-C-XS, UES-C-S, UES-C-M, UES-C-L) and shape (circular, rounded square)
* Employ a tool-free snap-fit mounting system
* Work with any Standards Glasses-compliant frame
* Can be ordered online and installed at home

==== For Consumers

1. **Initial Measurement**: Visit an optometrist for:
   * Prescription measurement
   * Pupillary distance (PD) measurement - recorded per UES size/shape chosen

2. **Choose Your Size & Shape**: Select a UES size and shape (e.g. UES-C-S) based on personal preference and face fit - not the prescription. Each consumer may own lenses in more than one size/shape.

3. **Lens Purchase**: Order UES lenses (in your chosen size/shape) from:
   * Online retailers
   * Optical laboratories
   * Traditional optometrists
   * Direct from lens manufacturers

4. **Frame Selection**: Choose any UES frame in your size/shape:
   * Buy multiple frames for different occasions
   * Replace broken frames without replacing lenses
   * Try fashion frames without commitment

5. **At-Home Installation**: 
   * Snap lenses into frames (no tools required)
   * Takes <30 seconds per lens
   * Visual confirmation of proper engagement

==== For the Industry

===== Lens Manufacturers

* Produce lenses in standard sizes instead of fully custom
* Batch production reduces unit costs
* Potential for inventory management (common sizes stocked)
* Market opportunity in direct-to-consumer sales

===== Frame Manufacturers

* Design frames around standard apertures
* Differentiate through style, materials, and features (not lens shape)
* Lower entry barrier for new brands
* Potential subscription models (frame-of-the-month)

===== Optometrists (person)

* Focus on professional services: eye health, prescription, fitting
* Establish office without sales / front

===== Optical Labs

* Simplified production with standardized sizes
* Faster turnaround times
* Reduced waste from mis-cuts
* Higher volume potential

*No universal consumer standard exists* across brands and manufacturers.

=== Why It Hasn't Been Done

1. **Fashion vs. Function Tension**
   * Eyewear is a fashion item
   * Brands differentiate through unique lens shapes
   * Standardization perceived as limiting design freedom

2. **Industry Fragmentation**
   * Thousands of frame manufacturers
   * No coordinating body for consumer eyewear standards
   * Dominated by few large companies (Luxottica, Safilo) with no incentive to standardize

3. **Optical Complexity**
   * Different frame geometries require different base curves
   * Concern that fixed sizes would compromise optical quality
   * True for extreme wraparound or highly tilted frames, but most fashion frames fall within acceptable range

4. **Patent Landscape**
   * Various patents on interchangeable lens systems
   * Creates legal uncertainty for new entrants
   * No one has published an open, royalty-free standard

=== Impact Opportunity

==== Scale of the Problem

An estimated **2.2 billion people** worldwide live with a vision impairment, of whom at least 1 billion have a condition that could be corrected with prescription eyewear but goes untreated.
Cost and logistics - not clinical complexity - are the primary barriers.

* ~1 billion people lack access to affordable prescription eyewear
* Uncorrected refractive errors are the leading cause of vision impairment globally
* Average replacement cycle for eyewear: 2-3 years; for children, frames may need replacing annually due to growth or damage
* In low- and middle-income countries, a single pair of custom glasses can represent weeks of income

==== Who Benefits

UES is an open infrastructure standard - like USB or Bluetooth, its value comes from universal adoption.
Everyone in the eyewear ecosystem benefits when lenses and frames from any brand work together.

**Consumers** - any consumer, anywhere in the world:

* Buy frames and lenses from different brands without compatibility concerns
* Replace a broken frame without replacing lenses, and vice versa
* Own multiple frame styles for the same pair of lenses
* Order replacement parts online without a new clinical visit

**Manufacturers**:

* Compete on design, materials, and quality - not on proprietary lock-in
* Access a larger addressable market: any UES frame works with any UES lens
* Lower barrier to entry for new brands

**Optometrists and Optical Labs**:

* Focus on clinical and professional services rather than frame sales
* Simplified lens production with standardised sizes; reduced waste from mis-cuts

**Public Health and Humanitarian Programs**:

* Standard size codes allow pre-positioning of lens stock in schools, clinics, and disaster-relief supply chains
* Donated or subsidised lenses serve many individuals without individual manufacturing lead time
* Particularly impactful for children, who outgrow or damage frames frequently - a new frame does not require new lenses

== Business Model

=== Open Standard with a non-profit backing

The UES initiative operates as an **open, royalty-free specification** governed by a non-profit association (_Associação sem fins lucrativos_, the "Association").

==== The specification

The technical specification is published publicly and is free to implement without royalties or membership fees.
Any manufacturer may read, implement, and ship products based on it at no cost, provided they do not use the official UES logo or trademarks.

==== Participation options

There are two paths to logo rights:

**Logo Licensee - 250 €/year**

* Right to use the UES logo and trademarks on compliant products, packaging, and marketing;
* Relationship is purely contractual via the Trademark/Logo Licence Agreement; no governance role.

**Membership - 1,000 €/year**

All rights of the Logo Licensee, plus:

* Membership in the association.

==== The logo as a conformance mark

.UES conformance mark to be used on compliant products
image::logo-mark.svg[UES conformance mark,120,align="center"]

The Association holds the trademark over the UES logo, registered as an EU trade mark (EUTM) through the EUIPO in the following Nice Classification classes:

* **Class 9** - optical apparatus and instruments; spectacles, spectacle frames, lenses, and related eyewear components.
* **Class 42** - technical standardisation services; testing and quality certification relating to eyewear conformance standards.

The logo functions as a **conformance mark** - it signals to consumers that a product has been made in conformance with the specification.

To use the mark, a manufacturer must:

. Self-certify conformance by running the published conformity test and keeping records;
. Join as a **Member** or register as a **Logo Licensee** by signing the Trademark/Logo Licence Agreement and paying the corresponding annual fee.

There is no separate per-product or per-model logo fee under either path.
Membership and logo rights lapse if the annual fee is not renewed.
The Association may request self-test documentation or production samples at any time, and may revoke logo use rights if products are found to be non-compliant.

==== How the two interact

[cols="1,2", options="header"]
|===
|Layer
|Who controls it

|Specification (technical standard)
|Public domain / open licence - anyone may implement freely

|Conformance conformity test
|Published by the Association - defines what "compliant" means in practice

|Logo / conformance mark
|Available to Members and Logo Licensees; requires self-certified conformance and signed Trademark/Logo Licence Agreement; revocable on non-conformance or lapsed fee
|===

In plain terms: *build a conforming product, self-test it, sign the licence, choose your path (Member or Logo Licensee), use the mark.*
The Association's role is to maintain the specification and conformity test, hold the trademark registration, and act on credible reports of misuse of the mark.

==== Fee structure rationale

Both fees are intentionally modest compared with complex digital standards, reflecting the lower technical and operational complexity of a mechanical interface.

* **Member fee** - covers non-profit operating expenses (spec maintenance, website, registry, trademarks, governance) and ensures only serious manufacturers shape the long-term direction of the standard.
* **Logo Licensee fee** - provides a low-barrier path for brands that need logo rights but not governance participation; still acts as a seriousness filter and contributes modestly toward trademark upkeep.

=== How to Use the UES Mark: Step-by-Step

The following flow describes how a manufacturer obtains and maintains the right to apply the UES mark to their products.

==== 1. Build a conforming product

Obtain the link:../spec/technical-spec.adoc[UES Technical Specification] and implement the interface geometry for your product type.

==== 2. Self-certify conformance

Run the conformance conformity test defined in the specification against a representative production sample.
No external laboratory is required.
If the product fails, correct the design and re-test.
Keep test records on file; the Association may request them.

==== 3. Choose your path and pay the annual fee

Download the Trademark/Logo Licence Agreement from the Association's website and complete the required fields (company name, country, product type, declared size code(s)).
Choose one of two paths:

* **Member** - logo rights on all declared compliant products, plus voting rights and participation in governance and working groups.
* **Logo Licensee** - logo rights on declared compliant products; no voting rights or governance role.

Pay the corresponding annual fee and submit the signed agreement to the Association.
Upon acknowledgement, your logo rights are active.

==== 4. Apply the mark and maintain compliance

Once a member in good standing, apply the UES mark to compliant products per the Logo Usage Guidelines.
Renew the annual membership fee each year to keep your rights active.
Re-testing is only required if the interface geometry changes materially.

=== Risks and Mitigation

==== Patent Infringement

*Impact:* High - could block the entire project.

Existing patents on interchangeable lens systems create legal uncertainty.
A thorough freedom-to-operate (FTO) analysis must be conducted before publication.
Where blocking patents are found, the design should be adapted to work around them.
Proactive defensive publication of the specification creates prior art and prevents future patent encumbrance by third parties.

==== Industry Resistance

*Impact:* High - no adoption means failure.

Large incumbents (Luxottica, Safilo) have no financial incentive to standardize and may actively resist.
The strategy is to begin with independent frame and lens manufacturers who stand to gain from a level playing field.
Demonstrating real consumer demand and cost savings early builds the case for broader adoption.
An open, royalty-free model removes the main commercial objection.

==== Optical Quality Concerns

*Impact:* Medium - could limit adoption or credibility.

Optometrists and lens manufacturers may argue that fixed sizes compromise prescription accuracy, particularly for high-power or custom base-curve requirements.
Mitigation requires rigorous independent testing across a range of prescriptions, and early engagement with optometric associations to define acceptable tolerances within the specification.

==== Consumer Skepticism

*Impact:* Medium - risks slow initial adoption.

Consumers accustomed to fully custom lenses may distrust a standardized system.
Pilot programs with money-back guarantees lower the perceived risk.
Endorsements from independent optometrists and sustainability advocates help build credibility.
Clear installation demonstrations (video, in-store) reduce friction at the point of purchase.

==== Manufacturing Complexity

*Impact:* Low - may increase unit cost marginally.

Introducing a new interface geometry adds tooling requirements for manufacturers.
This is mitigated by involving manufacturers early in the specification process so tolerances are achievable with existing production lines.
The long-term volume benefits of batch production outweigh the initial tooling cost.

==== Competing Standards

*Impact:* Medium - market fragmentation would undermine the network effect.

A rival proprietary standard from a large brand could split the market.
First-mover advantage is critical: publishing an open specification quickly establishes prior art and a reference implementation.
Open licensing and an inclusive governance model make it unattractive for competitors to fork a separate standard.
